## Working title

**《植物图谱与视觉分类：从自然史图像到知识秩序》**

(English working title, for reference only: *Botanical Plates and Visual Taxonomy: From Natural-History Images to the Order of Knowledge*.)

The title fixes three things at once:

1. **Subject.** Botanical / natural-history images, not generic "science" or "art".
2. **Operation.** Visual taxonomy — the act of classifying by looking — rather than molecular biology or ecological fieldwork.
3. **Argument.** A journey from observation to public knowledge, not a static display of pretty plates.

---

## Core visitor question

> 一张植物图像如何从观察记录，变成分类、教学、收藏和公共知识的一部分？

(How does a plant image travel from a record of observation into part of classification, teaching, collection, and public knowledge?)

This question is what every section, artifact card, and glossary term should be answerable against. If a piece of content cannot be traced back to this question, it does not belong in this exhibition.

## Proposed sections

Four sections. Each section is a *step* in the journey from observation to public knowledge, not a *topic* in isolation.

### Section 1 — 观察：图像如何抓住植物特征

- **Purpose.** Show how an illustrator or fieldworker chooses what to look at, and what to leave out. The image as a *decision*, not a copy.
- **Possible artifact types.** Botanical plate (hand-colored engraving); field sketch (later worked up into a plate).
- **Visitor takeaway.** A botanical illustration is a compressed argument, not a neutral photograph.
- **Source requirements.** Public-domain or open-access plate from a pre-1928 work, OR a project-generated diagram that names the features it is highlighting.
- **Rights notes.** Pre-1928 plates default to public domain in most jurisdictions, but **the source page must say so explicitly** before the asset enters the candidate set. Do not infer from date alone.

### Section 2 — 分类：图像如何服务命名与秩序

- **Purpose.** Show how an illustration becomes part of a taxonomic system — a binomial name, a family, an order. The image as a *node* in a naming system.
- **Possible artifact types.** Plate annotated with binomial; herbarium sheet (specimen + label); taxonomic diagram (project-generated, comparing two related species).
- **Visitor takeaway.** Classification is visual: the *features* an illustrator chose to emphasize are exactly the features a taxonomist uses to sort species.
- **Source requirements.** Public-domain or open-access herbarium sheet from an open-access herbarium (e.g., a Smithsonian Open Access specimen), with a clear rights statement and a stable identifier.
- **Rights notes.** Specimen photos are usually institutional property; rights must be checked per record. Project-generated comparison diagrams are owned by the project and must be clearly labeled as such.

### Section 3 — 复制：书籍、版画与数字化如何改变传播

- **Purpose.** Show how the same image is multiplied — engraved, lithographed, photographed, scanned, digitized — and how each medium changes what the image can do.
- **Possible artifact types.** Digitized book page (showing the plate in its published context); side-by-side comparison of plate vs. scan (project-generated diagram).
- **Visitor takeaway.** A reproduction is not neutral. Each medium adds or removes something — color, scale, accessibility, shareability.
- **Source requirements.** Digitized book page from a clearly-rights-cleared digital library (e.g., BHL); the book as a whole must have a clear license, not just the platform.
- **Rights notes.** "Digitized by Google / Internet Archive / BHL" is *not* the same as "rights cleared for reuse". The book's rights statement must be cited separately from the digitizer.

### Section 4 — 再组织：数字馆藏如何让图像重新连接

- **Purpose.** Show how a digital collection re-links images that the printed book separated — same species from two herbaria, same plate from two editions, same specimen across two records.
- **Possible artifact types.** Collection record screenshot (with rights noted as platform-only); project-generated network diagram showing how records connect.
- **Visitor takeaway.** A digital collection is not just a warehouse. It is a *re-linking engine*: the same image gains new neighbors when the catalog gains new fields.
- **Source requirements.** Project-generated diagrams are the primary artifact here; platform screenshots are **avoided** unless the underlying image's rights are independently cleared.
- **Rights notes.** Platform screenshots fall into risk category 3 ("platform screenshot ambiguity") in the rights-risk register — typically **High by default**. Project-generated diagrams are preferred.

## Artifact candidates

The following artifact *types* may appear in v4.3 build. v4.0 only lists the types; no real images are downloaded or imported in v4.0.

- **botanical plate** — A printed illustration of one or more plant species, typically hand-colored engraving or lithograph.
- **herbarium sheet** — A pressed, dried, mounted plant specimen with a printed or handwritten label.
- **digitized book page** — A scan of a single page from a digitized book, showing one or more plates in their published context.
- **collection record screenshot** — A screenshot of a collection record page (object metadata + thumbnail) from an institution's catalog.
- **project-generated comparison diagram** — A diagram generated by the project itself, comparing two plates, two sheets, or two editions.

**Real images are not imported in v4.0.** v4.1 will research candidate assets; v4.2 will audit them; v4.3 will only place assets that pass both gates.

## Glossary candidates

At least the following ten terms will appear in the rendered glossary. The actual rendered glossary may include more; these are the floor.

1. **botanical illustration** — A detailed, usually hand-rendered image of a plant made for scientific or documentary purposes.
2. **taxonomy** — The practice and theory of naming, defining, and classifying groups of organisms by shared characteristics.
3. **herbarium** — A curated collection of preserved plant specimens, typically pressed, dried, and mounted on sheets.
4. **specimen** — A single preserved plant (or part of a plant) used as a reference point in a herbarium or collection.
5. **plate** — A single full-page illustration in a botanical or natural-history book, usually printed separately from the text and inserted at binding.
6. **engraving** — A printmaking technique in which a design is cut into a hard surface; the dominant technique for botanical plates before the late 19th century.
7. **digitization** — The conversion of an analog artifact (book, plate, sheet) into a digital surrogate, typically by scanning or photographing.
8. **metadata** — Structured descriptive information about an artifact: title, creator, date, institution, identifier, rights.
9. **collection record** — A catalog entry describing a single object in a collection, including its metadata and (often) a thumbnail or image.
10. **public domain** — A legal status in which a work is not covered by copyright, either because its term has expired, the creator released it, or it was never eligible.

Additional candidates (optional, v4.3 may include if room): binomial nomenclature, type specimen, lithograph, color plate, finding aid, persistent identifier.

## Visitor takeaway (aggregate)

If a visitor reads only the hero and the 3-minute guide, they should leave with the following three things:

1. A botanical illustration is a *decision*, not a copy.
2. Classification is *visual*; the features an illustrator chose are the features a taxonomist uses.
3. A digital collection is a *re-linking engine*, not just a warehouse.

These three lines are the test against which the eventual curatorial essay is graded.
